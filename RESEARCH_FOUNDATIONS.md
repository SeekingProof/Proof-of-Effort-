## Abstract

Decades of writing process research have established that authentic text composition produces measurably distinct behavioral patterns reflecting the cognitive processes of planning, formulation, evaluation, and revision. These patterns — observable in pause structure, revision behavior, deletion characteristics, process variance, cursor navigation, and broader word processor interactions — are grounded in well-characterized cognitive mechanisms and are stable across diverse writer populations. The Proof of Effort (PoE) system developed by Khepri Humetric Solutions operationalizes this body of research as a privacy-preserving, cryptographically verifiable attestation framework. PoE captures the full behavioral record of a composition session, applies transformations that render the data non-identifiable, and produces a portable attestation artifact encoding a NEOHE (Non-biometric Evidence of Human Effort) score that quantifies the degree to which the observed behavioral patterns are consistent with empirically calibrated reference patterns associated with authentic human composition. Rather than issuing detection verdicts, PoE generates durable, verifiable evidence of the compositional process itself. This paper grounds PoE's methodology in peer-reviewed writing process research, establishes the cognitive basis of the behavioral dimensions PoE measures, documents the discriminative validity of those dimensions, and articulates the architectural properties that distinguish PoE from detection-oriented approaches.

## 1. The Problem: Authenticity of Cognitive Engagement in Written Composition

The increasing availability of generative artificial intelligence has created a structural challenge for institutions that rely on written composition as evidence of learning, competence, or professional capability. Existing approaches to authenticity verification focus predominantly on the written product — examining text for statistical signatures of machine generation or matching against known sources. These approaches share a fundamental limitation: they assess the artifact rather than the process that produced it. As generative AI systems produce increasingly naturalistic text, product-based approaches face an inherent ceiling on discriminative power.

A substantively different approach is possible. Decades of writing process research have established that authentic composition leaves measurable behavioral traces in the process of text production itself — traces that are independent of the linguistic characteristics of the final product and that reflect cognitive engagement in ways that cannot be replicated by reproduction of externally generated text. These behavioral traces encode the cognitive work of planning, formulating, evaluating, and revising — processes that operate differently when a writer is composing original content versus reproducing content that has already been generated.

The PoE system developed by Khepri Humetric Solutions operationalizes these research findings as a practical attestation infrastructure. PoE captures behavioral telemetry during composition sessions, applies privacy-preserving transformations that render the data non-identifiable, derives attestation metrics from the resulting derivative behavioral data, and produces a cryptographically sealed attestation artifact. The artifact encodes a NEOHE score — a quantified measurement of the degree to which the observed behavioral patterns are consistent with empirically calibrated reference patterns associated with authentic human composition. The score does not constitute a verdict or classification. PoE does not issue determinations of authorship or verdicts of inauthenticity; it provides a quantified, verifiable, and portable evidence record of the behavioral characteristics of a composition session.

## 2. Research Foundations: Behavioral Signals of Cognitive Engagement

The behavioral dimensions PoE measures are grounded in a well-established body of peer-reviewed research in writing process science, cognitive psychology, and educational data mining. The core claim is not that PoE detects transcription — it is that authentic writing produces a characteristic behavioral record that reflects genuine cognitive engagement, and that this record can be measured, preserved, and attested. The following sections establish the cognitive basis of the behavioral dimensions PoE measures, and then document their discriminative validity.

### 2.1 The Cognitive Architecture of Text Production

The foundational framework for understanding the behavioral record of writing is the model of text production described by Galbraith and Baaijen (2019), drawing on Chenoweth and Hayes (2003). The model distinguishes four recursively interacting sub-processes: the Proposer (idea generation and high-level planning), the Translator (converting ideas to linguistic strings), the Transcriber (converting linguistic strings to physical text), and the Evaluator/Reviser (monitoring produced text and triggering correction when problems are identified). Each sub-process produces measurably distinct behavioral signatures in the temporal record of text production. The recursive interaction of these sub-processes — not their sequential execution — is what generates the characteristic behavioral variability of authentic composition.

Galbraith and Baaijen (2019) identify what they call the alignment problem: observable behavioral measures are multiply determined, meaning a given pause or revision event cannot be unambiguously attributed to a single cognitive sub-process. As they state directly: "Keystroke logging provides a detailed record of the process of writing as it unfolds in time. However, by themselves, the measures it provides — pauses, bursts and revisions — do not provide an unambiguous interpretation of the cognitive processes involved." PoE addresses this by deriving attestation metrics from multiple independent behavioral dimensions simultaneously rather than relying on any single measure — an approach grounded in the same principle the research literature identifies as necessary for valid inference.

### 2.2 Behavioral Signals of Cognitive Engagement

Research across multiple independent programs has established that specific behavioral dimensions of the writing record encode cognitive engagement. Breetvelt, Van den Bergh, and Rijlaarsdam (1994) found that the type and timing of process activities — including rereading, evaluating, and revising — explained a substantial proportion of variance in text quality, establishing that the behavioral record of composition is not incidental to cognitive engagement but constitutive of it. Leijten and Van Waes (2013) established the foundational methodological framework: keystroke logging captures every keystroke, cursor movement, and mouse interaction during composition, and the resulting record can be analyzed across multiple dimensions to characterize the cognitive activity underlying text production.

Pause structure is among the most thoroughly characterized of these dimensions. Galbraith and Baaijen (2019), drawing on Baaijen, Galbraith, and de Glopper (2012), show that pause duration distributions are multimodal — with distinct clusters corresponding to word retrieval (mean approximately 330 ms), phrase boundary planning (mean approximately 735 ms), and higher-level content planning or reflection (mean approximately 2,697 ms). Single summary statistics misrepresent this structure; distributional analysis is required to capture its cognitive content. Between-sentence pauses are the most directly interpretable category, representing deliberate content planning. Research by Medimorec and Risko (2017) has examined pause location relative to text boundaries, with findings consistent with the expectation that different boundary types reflect different levels of cognitive processing. Conijn, Roeser, and van Zaanen (2019) found that average inter-keystroke interval is stable across tasks of different cognitive demand, while distributional and location-based pause measures vary — establishing that it is the structure of the pause record, not its aggregate rate, that carries cognitive signal.

Revision and deletion behavior is the behavioral trace of the Evaluator/Reviser sub-process. Leijten and Van Waes (2013) established the foundational interpretation widely adopted in the field: revisions indicate a discrepancy between the writer's intentions and the text produced, making them a direct trace of evaluative cognitive activity. Galbraith and Baaijen (2019) formalize the distinction between production bursts terminated by planning pauses and those terminated by evaluator events, finding that burst type and length encode different cognitive functions. Sinharay, Zhang, and Deane (2019) found in large-scale writing assessment data that revision-related features — burst count and burst length — were associated with writing quality scores, with results suggesting that engaged writers show characteristic revision patterns, though Conijn, Cook, van Zaanen, and Van Waes (2022) caution that the relationship between keystroke features and quality is task-sensitive and should not be overgeneralized.

Process variance — the degree to which production rate fluctuates across the session — is itself a cognitive signal. Authentic composition is not temporally uniform; the recursive interplay of planning, formulation, and evaluation produces characteristic variation. Research by Oliveira, Conijn, De Barba, Trezise, van Zaanen, and Kennedy (2020) has examined the relationship between cognitive load and keystroke behavior across tasks, with results consistent with the interpretation that process variance reflects cognitive state. Tian, Crossley, and Van Waes (2025) found in the KLiCKe corpus across approximately 5,000 sessions that lower process variance was associated with more engaged, higher-quality composition. Guo, Zhang, Deane, and Bennett (2019) modeled writing process data as sequences of states and found that the sequential structure of state transitions — not just the frequency of individual states — encodes compositional engagement. Conijn et al. (2022) found that the relationship between behavioral features and writing quality changes across the course of a session, confirming that temporal evolution of the record carries information beyond session-level aggregations.

Cursor navigation and scrolling represent a further dimension. Research combining keystroke logging and eye tracking, including work by Leijten and Van Waes (2013) and related studies, has found that cursor key use and scrolling within the emerging text correspond to rereading behavior — the writer reading their own text produced so far — which is positively correlated with text quality and associated with the evaluative sub-process. These are not text input events, but they encode cognitive activity that is integral to the composition process.

### 2.3 Discriminative Validity: Behavioral Signals Distinguish Compositional States

The cognitive grounding of behavioral writing signals would be of limited practical value if the signals were not sufficiently discriminating to distinguish meaningfully different compositional states. The research literature establishes that they are.

The most direct evidence comes from Crossley, Tian, Holmes, Morris, and Choi (2024), who collected behavioral records from 500 authentic essay writers and 500 writers transcribing the same essays verbatim, then trained machine learning models on the resulting behavioral features. A random forest model achieved 99% classification accuracy. The key discriminating features were consistent with the cognitive model: authentic writers produced significantly more pauses before sentences and words, deleted significantly more words and characters, generated more revisions, and produced substantially more insertions. Transcribers, reproducing already-formulated content, showed near-linear production with minimal revision activity. As Crossley et al. (2024) characterize it, authentic writing is a dynamic process in which writers monitor and evaluate their output, while transcribed writing is much more linear. The product-to-process ratio — final text length relative to total characters produced — was substantially lower for authentic writers, reflecting the recursive correction characteristic of genuine composition.

This finding has a critical practical implication: it holds regardless of the quality of the reproduced content. A writer transcribing AI-generated text of high quality produces the same behavioral signature as a writer transcribing any other externally generated text. The behavioral record encodes the cognitive process, not the linguistic product — making it resistant to the AI quality improvement that undermines product-based detection approaches. Tian et al. (2025) replicated core findings across approximately 5,000 sessions in the KLiCKe corpus, providing large-scale confirmation that the behavioral dimensions the research identifies are stable and reproducible across diverse writer populations.

The discriminative validity finding is significant for PoE's purposes not because transcription detection is PoE's primary function, but because it establishes the magnitude of the behavioral signal. If the behavioral record of authentic composition is sufficiently distinct from reproduction to enable near-perfect classification, it carries substantial information about the cognitive process underlying text production — information that PoE captures, preserves, and attests.

## 3. Where PoE Diverges from Prior Research

The research summarized above establishes that behavioral signals of cognitive engagement in writing are real, measurable, and sufficiently discriminating to characterize compositional states. Prior research implementations have applied this finding primarily to detection and classification tasks. PoE addresses a different problem and operates under different constraints. The divergences are architectural, not empirical.

### 3.1 From Detection to Attestation

Prior research systems — including Crossley et al. (2024) and related work — are designed as detection systems: they receive a behavioral record and classify it as authentic or transcribed. Classification is the output. This is appropriate for the research questions those systems address. PoE addresses a different question: how can the behavioral evidence of authentic composition be captured, preserved, and verified in a portable, durable form that a writer can present to any interested party at any future time?

The output of PoE is not a classification but an attestation artifact: a cryptographically sealed record containing the derivative behavioral metrics from which the NEOHE score was computed, the scoring methodology, and a cryptographic integrity seal. The NEOHE score quantifies the degree to which the observed behavioral patterns are consistent with empirically calibrated reference patterns associated with authentic human composition — it is a measurement, not a verdict. Where PoE includes a transcription detection component, that component produces a separate probability-based finding using a corroborated signal architecture; this finding is distinct from the NEOHE score and is not the primary output of the system. The artifact is stateless to verify — any party with the artifact and the public verification key can confirm its integrity and recompute its score without communicating with PoE's infrastructure. This portability and verifiability have no equivalent in detection-oriented or prior attestation systems.

### 3.2 Privacy-Preserving Transformation

Prior research systems operate on raw behavioral data — exact timing values, specific key identities, cursor positions, and other directly observable measurements. This creates a tension with privacy: raw behavioral timing data at sufficient resolution is capable of identifying individuals. Monaco and Tappert (2016) demonstrated that a random delay of as little as 25 ms reduces identification accuracy by approximately 20%, and that delays of 500 ms are needed to halve identification accuracy — confirming that the sub-100ms timing data captured during keystroke logging is individually identifying at baseline. Killourhy and Maxion (2008) showed that typical Windows clock resolution of approximately 15 ms introduces approximately a 4.2% increase in equal-error rate, and that timing noise from any source degrades detector performance, with degradation becoming severe at resolutions of 50 ms and above. These findings together establish that raw timing data raises significant concerns under data protection frameworks including GDPR, FERPA, and institutional IRB requirements.

PoE applies privacy-preserving transformations to raw behavioral telemetry before any scoring or storage occurs. These transformations produce derivative metrics — distributional representations, categorical aggregations, and variance measures — that preserve the behavioral signal relevant to attestation while rendering the data non-identifiable at the individual level. The transformations are one-way: the original timing data cannot be recovered from the derivative metrics stored in the attestation artifact. This approach enables PoE to operate without collecting or storing biometric data, addressing the principal institutional privacy concern with behavioral monitoring systems.

### 3.3 Population-Level Calibration Without Individual Enrollment

Behavioral authentication systems require per-user enrollment: they compare a current behavioral sample against a stored baseline from the same individual. This requires collecting and retaining individually identifiable behavioral data, creating ongoing privacy exposure and logistical overhead incompatible with many institutional contexts.

PoE does not require enrollment. The attestation score is calibrated against population-level reference distributions — the aggregate behavioral characteristics of authentic human composition across a diverse reference population — rather than against any individual baseline. This approach is possible because the behavioral dimensions PoE measures are cognitively grounded properties that are stable across the population of authentic writers, not idiosyncratic individual signatures. The research literature supports this: the behavioral distinctions between authentic composition and transcription are population-level phenomena observable across diverse writer populations (Crossley et al., 2024; Tian et al., 2025).

### 3.4 Multi-Dimensional Probability Scoring

Prior detection systems typically aggregate behavioral features into a single machine learning classifier. While this approach maximizes classification accuracy on the training population, it produces an opaque result — a binary label or confidence score whose behavioral basis is not transparent to the verifier.

PoE computes NEOHE scores from multiple independent behavioral dimensions, each corresponding to a distinct aspect of the cognitive writing process identified in the research literature. The NEOHE score reflects the joint measurement of behavioral evidence across these dimensions and quantifies the degree to which observed patterns are consistent with empirically calibrated reference patterns associated with authentic human composition. This approach provides interpretive transparency — the behavioral basis of the score is documented in the artifact — and is grounded in the research principle that individual behavioral measures are multiply determined and that multi-dimensional evidence is more reliable than any single measure, as Galbraith and Baaijen (2019) articulate explicitly.

### 3.5 Extended Behavioral Capture Beyond Prior Research Implementations

Prior research implementations of keystroke logging capture text input events and their timing — keystrokes, inter-keystroke intervals, and the deletions and insertions that constitute revision activity. This is the behavioral record that the published literature has characterized and validated. PoE captures this record in their derivative forms, but extends it to a broader set of behavioral events that prior systems do not address.

The published research has established that user activity beyond the raw timing of text input — cursor navigation, scrolling, and mouse movement — is correlated with cognitive engagement. These events, though not text input, have been identified as proxies for rereading and evaluation behavior: research combining keystroke logging with eye tracking has found that cursor key use and scrolling correspond to reading one's own emerging text, positively correlated with text quality and evaluative engagement. They represent separate cognitive behaviors that often lead to revision and style decisions just as essential to composition, and denote metacognitive awareness about the text and its intended audience. Khepri's internal research shows that behavioral interactions beyond text input and cursor movement represent additional and more cognitively complex signals: the logic that establishes cursor movement as a proxy for cognitive engagement follows through to any and all user behaviors that impact the final text in terms of content, style, and format.

PoE accordingly captures formatting and styling events — paragraph alignment, indentation, list structure, character emphasis, and related word processor commands — as part of the behavioral record of a composition session. These events are not individually decisive as attestation signals, and no published research has characterized them as cognitive proxies for composition specifically. They are captured by PoE as additional behavioral dimensions whose presence, timing, and sequential relationship to text production events contribute to the fullness of the behavioral record. The derivative metrics PoE computes from this extended record are designed to be sensitive to the breadth of compositional engagement, not solely to the dimensions the published research has characterized.

## 4. PoE as a Novel Class of Behavioral Attestation

The research literature establishes that behavioral signatures of authentic composition are empirically real and discriminating. Prior systems have applied this finding to detection. PoE applies it to a structurally different problem: generating portable, verifiable, privacy-preserving evidence of cognitive engagement in written composition.

The Non-biometric Evidence of Human Effort (NEOHE) score produced by PoE represents a new data classification. It is distinct from biometric authentication — which identifies individuals — because PoE's data transformations are explicitly designed to preclude individual identification, and the scoring framework compares behavioral patterns to population norms rather than individual baselines. It is distinct from content provenance systems — which track asset history and transformation chains — because PoE measures process characteristics rather than content state. It is distinct from detection-oriented systems because its output is a portable, verifiable attestation artifact rather than an in-system classification.

NEOHE quantifies the degree to which the behavioral record of a composition session is consistent with empirically calibrated reference patterns associated with authentic human composition, expressed as a score on a 0.0 to 1.0 scale. The score is a measurement of telemetry characteristics — not a verdict or classification. It is reproducible — independent verification of the same artifact always produces the same result — and portable — the artifact carries its own verification data and requires no ongoing relationship with PoE's infrastructure to verify, except insofar as necessary to recompute the score from any provided PoE data.

For institutional contexts, PoE addresses a gap that neither detection systems nor content provenance systems fill: the need for a durable, portable, privacy-preserving record that a writer can present as evidence of authentic compositional engagement. A student submitting a thesis, a professional submitting a report, a researcher submitting a manuscript can attach a PoE artifact as behavioral evidence of the compositional process — evidence that is grounded in peer-reviewed cognitive writing research, generated under privacy-preserving constraints, and verifiable by any recipient without institutional infrastructure.

## 5. Conclusion

Writing process research spanning multiple decades has established that authentic text composition produces a characteristic behavioral record reflecting the cognitive processes of planning, formulation, transcription, and revision. The behavioral signals encoded in this record — pause structure, revision activity, process variance, temporal rhythm, and cursor navigation — have been extensively characterized and have been shown to carry sufficient discriminative power to distinguish meaningfully different compositional states.

PoE operationalizes this body of research as a privacy-preserving, cryptographically verifiable attestation infrastructure. The derivative metrics PoE computes correspond to the behavioral dimensions identified in the research literature while being expressed in a transformed, non-identifiable form. The attestation artifacts PoE produces are portable and stateless to verify, enabling a new class of process-based evidence that complements and extends existing approaches to academic and professional integrity.

PoE does not claim to determine authorship or issue verdicts of inauthenticity. It provides quantified, verifiable behavioral evidence — grounded in peer-reviewed research, generated under rigorous privacy-preserving constraints — that the composition session producing a given text exhibited behavioral patterns consistent with authentic cognitive engagement in writing.
